\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Section~\ref{sec:background} surveyed the prior art in narrative form;
this section consolidates the comparison into structured form
(Tables~\ref{tab:expressivity} and~\ref{tab:tooling}), then discusses
the closest peers and articulates how Yuho positions against each.

The honest reading of the matrix is that no two systems target the
same point in the design space. Catala has the right defaults but
no penalty algebra. lam4 has the right deontic core but no
statute-level structural primitives. LexScript shares Yuho's
tree-sitter-driven shape but has a thinner element model. LDOC has
illustrations because Word renders them naturally, but it is a Word
authoring tool first and a DSL second. Akoma Ntoso preserves
structure faithfully and is the only competitor with real
amendment-lineage support, but stops where semantics begins.
LegalRuleML adds a deontic layer above the Akoma Ntoso markup, but
does not commit to a fixed structural shape for an offence.

The columns where Yuho is alone in claiming first-class support ---
penalty algebra, burden qualifiers, causal/temporal edges, and the
combination of all of the above on a statute-shaped top level --- are
exactly the columns that fall out of taking the Penal Code's drafting
conventions seriously. Where Yuho overlaps with a peer, the design
borrows directly: Catala for the priority semantics, lam4 for the
named-norm references planned under \textsf{G10}, LexScript for the
tree-sitter pipeline shape and the Tarjan-SCC analysis, LDOC for the
DOCX authoring loop. The borrowings are explicit; we do not claim
novelty on the borrowed pieces.

\subsection{Catala}
\label{subsec:rel_catala}

Catala~\cite{catala2021} is the most influential reference for this
work. The two systems disagree on top-level shape (scope vs statute)
but agree that the right semantics for legal exceptions is prioritised
default rules. We adopt the priority semantics directly: each Yuho
\texttt{exception} block carries an integer \texttt{priority} field,
and compilation to Z3 follows Catala's prioritised-rewriting algorithm
in shape if not in proof. Catala also formalises a soundness theorem
for its rewriting system; we do not, but we treat Catala's theorem
as the warrant that the priority semantics is well-defined and behaves
the way drafted statutes are read.

The disagreement on top-level shape matters in practice. Catala's
scope is a callable function: it takes inputs (facts about the case),
returns outputs (the legal conclusion), and composes via scope-call
sites. This is the right shape for tax code, where a tax-credit
calculation is a function from taxpayer-facts to tax-owed. It is the
wrong shape for a Penal Code section, where the section bundles
definitions, elements, illustrations, and case law that are not
function arguments. We discuss scope composition as a future-work
layer (\S\ref{sec:limitations}) that adds Catala-style scope-call
semantics on top of Yuho's statute blocks rather than replacing them.

\subsection{lam4}
\label{subsec:rel_lam4}

lam4~\cite{lam4} originates from the SMU Centre for Computational
Law and is, like Yuho, a Singapore-context legal DSL. It is the
closest jurisdictional peer; the systems target different statute
families (lam4: contracts and regulations; Yuho: penal code) but
share an institutional context that makes comparison easy.

lam4's distinctive primitive is the named-norm reference and the
\texttt{IS\_INFRINGED} predicate: a contract clause can predicate
on whether another named norm has been infringed, which composes
cleanly into multi-clause reasoning. This composes directly into
our planned \textsf{G10} cross-section semantic resolver: a Yuho
section like \texttt{statute 107 abetment} should be able to
predicate on whether a different section's offence elements are
satisfied, and the lam4 \texttt{IS\_INFRINGED} shape is the right
syntactic surface. We expect to import the predicate name and
semantics, with credit, when \textsf{G10} lands.

\subsection{LexScript}
\label{subsec:rel_lexscript}

LexScript~\cite{lexscript} is the closest tooling-shaped peer. It
shares Yuho's tree-sitter pipeline, multi-target transpile shape,
and statute-shaped top-level grammar. The differences are in depth,
not in approach: LexScript has a thinner element model (no burden
qualifier, no causal-link edges, no Catala-style exception priority)
and a broader cross-corpus posture (multiple statute families at
shallow coverage rather than one at full coverage).

The most directly portable LexScript idea is the Tarjan-SCC analysis
over the statute reference graph. We borrow it under \textsf{G10}:
once the cross-section resolver lands, an SCC pass will catch
unreachable exception branches and cyclic cross-references that the
plain parser cannot detect. The classification of LexScript as a
``thinner-grammar / broader-coverage'' alternative to Yuho's
``richer-grammar / single-family-coverage'' is meant descriptively;
the two systems make different bets on the right place to be on the
expressivity-coverage tradeoff.

\subsection{Akoma Ntoso and LegalRuleML}
\label{subsec:rel_akn}

Akoma Ntoso~\cite{akomantoso} and LegalRuleML~\cite{legalruleml}
are the OASIS-standardised XML schemas for, respectively, legislative
structural markup and legal rule representation. They are not direct
competitors to Yuho: their job is archival exchange and inter-system
interoperability, not executable encoding.

The constructive constraint that these standards impose on Yuho is
that we should be at least as faithful on the structural axis. Akoma
Ntoso is the only competitor with first-class amendment-lineage
support (its \texttt{<modifies>} element family captures every
gazetted amendment with its own metadata), and we treat that as the
benchmark our \texttt{effective}/\texttt{repealed}/\texttt{subsumes}/
\texttt{amends} clauses are obliged to match. We expect to ship a
Yuho-to-Akoma-Ntoso transpile target as future work; the converse
direction is harder because Akoma Ntoso does not commit to a fixed
shape for an offence definition, so an Akoma-Ntoso-to-Yuho lifting
would have to reconstruct missing semantics from the markup.

LegalRuleML is more semantic than Akoma Ntoso: it has deontic
operators (obligation, prohibition, permission) and isomorphic
mappings to several rule languages. It does not commit to a
prioritised-default semantics in the Catala sense, and it does not
have a notion of penalty algebra or illustration blocks. Where
Yuho's deontic operators (\texttt{obligation} / \texttt{prohibition}
/ \texttt{permission} elements) overlap with LegalRuleML's, the
mapping is straightforward; the rest of Yuho's structural surface
falls outside LegalRuleML's scope.

\subsection{LDOC}
\label{subsec:rel_ldoc}

LDOC~\cite{ldoc} prioritises the authoring loop: lawyers draft in
Word and the system extracts structured semantics. This is the
opposite of Yuho's source-of-truth posture: Yuho treats the
\texttt{.yh} file in version control as canonical and DOCX as one
of six generated outputs, while LDOC treats the Word document as
canonical and downstream renderings as derived. The two postures
target different audiences --- LDOC for practitioners who already
work in Word, Yuho for systems-and-DSL-friendly contributors --- and
both are coherent.

The constructive borrowing from LDOC is the DOCX transpile target.
A Yuho-encoded section that renders cleanly to a Word document is
a useful artefact for the practitioner audience, and the in-progress
Microsoft Word add-in (\S\ref{subsec:editors}) is meant to bridge
the two postures: a lawyer can browse Yuho-encoded sections from
inside Word, insert formatted statute text via the add-in, and read
the canonical Yuho file as needed without leaving the document.

\subsection{Wider context}
\label{subsec:rel_wider}

Several lines of work outside the legal-DSL bracket inform Yuho's
design without being direct comparators. Tree-sitter~\cite{treesitter}
provides the incremental parsing infrastructure shared with
LexScript and a growing number of other DSLs. The Language Server
Protocol matured into the de-facto integration point between
editors and language tooling, used by every modern legal DSL we
surveyed. The Model Context Protocol~\cite{mcp2024}, introduced in
2024, provides a parallel surface for AI clients; we are aware of
no other legal DSL that ships an MCP server, though we expect
several will in the next year.

The verification stack borrows from the broader formal-methods
literature. Z3~\cite{demoura2008z3} and Alloy~\cite{jackson2002alloy}
are well-established tools whose application to legal reasoning has
been demonstrated since the 2000s; our use of them is engineering
rather than research, and we make no claims of solver-side novelty.
