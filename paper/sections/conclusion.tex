\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented Yuho, a statute-shaped domain-specific language, and used
it to encode the entire Singapore Penal Code 1871: \statRawSections{}
sections, \statLOnePass{}/\statRawSections{} passing parse-and-build at
the L1+L2 coverage tiers, and \statLThreePass{} carrying a structured
human-fidelity stamp at the strictest L3 tier (\S\ref{subsec:coverage_eval}).
The exercise surfaced a catalogue of fourteen grammar gaps
(\S\ref{subsec:gaps}), distributed across ten genuine grammar
limitations that we resolved in the parser and four diagnostic-layer
issues that we resolved by adding fidelity linters. The result is a
complete, queryable, machine-checkable encoding of a production penal
code, plus the tooling --- a tree-sitter parser, an immutable Python
AST, six transpilers, a Z3-and-Alloy verification hookup, a Language
Server, a Model Context Protocol server, and a VS Code extension ---
that turns the encoding into an executable artefact rather than a
paper exercise.

The longer-term thesis is that \emph{statute-as-source-of-truth} is a
viable posture for legal infrastructure. The encoded artefact is what
the lawyer, the tooling, and the verification stack all read;
downstream renderings (controlled English, \LaTeX{}, Mermaid, DOCX)
are generated, not authored. We do not claim that the \statRawSections{}
encoded sections constitute publication-ready legal advice; the
limitations on common-law-doctrine modelling, precedent integration,
and external counsel review (\S\ref{sec:limitations}) are explicit
about what the artefact is and is not. We do claim that the gap
between a drafted penal section and an executable encoding is
narrower than the existing literature suggests, and that closing it
for one statute family at full coverage is mostly an engineering
exercise once the grammar is right.

\paragraph{Future work.} Three concrete strands. First, the
\textsf{G10} cross-section semantic resolver, which lifts the
already-parsed \texttt{referencing} / \texttt{subsumes} / \texttt{amends}
clauses into a real reference graph and unlocks LSP goto-definition,
the LexScript-borrowed Tarjan-SCC analysis, and the lam4-borrowed
\texttt{IS\_INFRINGED} predicate. Second, cross-jurisdiction
generalisation: encoding a 50-section sample of the Indian Penal Code
to test grammar fit and tooling reuse across the wider Anglo-Indian
penal-code family. Third, deeper precedent integration: lifting
case-law holding strings into structured constraints that modify
element burden qualifiers when the matching predicate fires.
A fourth, more speculative strand is scope composition in the
Catala~\cite{catala2021} sense, layered on top of Yuho's statute
blocks rather than replacing them, which would let abetment-style
sections (s107--s120) wrap arbitrary base offences as functions.

\paragraph{Artefact availability.} The Yuho compiler, encoded library,
and tooling are available under an open-source licence at
\url{https://github.com/gongahkia/yuho}. The paper itself, including
the build pipeline that injects coverage statistics into this very
manuscript at compile time, is in the same repository under
\texttt{paper/}.
