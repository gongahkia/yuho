We presented Yuho, a statute-shaped DSL, and used it to encode the
entire Singapore Penal Code 1871 ($\statRawSections{}$ sections,
$\statLOnePass{}$ passing parse-and-build, $\statLThreePass{}$ stamped
at the strictest fidelity tier). The exercise surfaced fourteen
grammar gaps, all either resolved in the grammar or rerouted to the
diagnostic layer, and produced a complete, queryable, machine-checkable
encoding of a working penal code.

The longer-term thesis is that statute-as-source-of-truth is a viable
posture for legal infrastructure: the encoded artefact is what a
lawyer, a tool, and a verifier all read; downstream rendering (English,
\LaTeX{}, DOCX) is generated, not authored. We do not claim the
\statRawSections{} encoded sections are publication-ready legal advice.
We do claim that the gap between drafted statute and executable artefact
is smaller than commonly assumed --- and that closing it is mostly an
engineering exercise once the grammar is right.

Future work has three concrete strands: (1) the G10 inter-section
semantic hookup, which unlocks reference-graph reachability and named-norm
references; (2) cross-jurisdiction generalisation, starting with the
Indian Penal Code; and (3) deeper precedent integration, lifting
holding strings into structured constraints over the element graph.
