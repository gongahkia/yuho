# EIC Review Report

**Reviewer**: Editor-in-Chief (PL + Legal Informatics)
**Manuscript**: Yuho: A Domain-Specific Language for Encoding the Singapore Penal Code as Executable Statute
**Mode**: Read-only editorial review (arXiv-preprint calibration)

## 1. Summary of Submission

The paper introduces *Yuho*, a tree-sitter-based DSL for encoding statutes as executable, machine-checkable artefacts, and reports on a proof-of-concept encoding of the Singapore Penal Code 1871 at full coverage (524/524 sections at L1+L2; 524/524 carrying an L3 human-stamped fidelity audit). The core claims, articulated in `\sec:intro`, are: (i) a statute-shaped grammar with first-class primitives for typed elements (actus reus / mens rea / circumstance / deontic), penalty algebra, Catala-style prioritised exceptions, illustrations, amendment lineage, and case-law anchors; (ii) an end-to-end toolchain (Python AST, six transpilers, Z3+Alloy verification, LSP, MCP server, VS Code extension); and (iii) an empirical catalogue of fourteen "grammar gaps" (G1--G14) surfaced by mass-encoding, partitioned into ten genuine grammar limitations and four diagnostic-layer issues. The paper positions Yuho against Catala, lam4, LexScript, LDOC, Akoma Ntoso, and LegalRuleML, and frames the work as an engineering-density argument: closing the gap from drafted statute to executable artefact is "mostly an engineering exercise once the grammar is right" (`\sec:intro`, `\sec:conclusion`).

## 2. Editorial Assessment

- **Journal fit (which venues this could target)**: Best primary fit is **Onward! Essays** (the engineering-density thesis and statute-as-source-of-truth posture are essay-shaped) or **The Programming Journal** (TPJ, for the language-design + tooling combination). **ICAIL** and **JURIX** are plausible alternative homes given the corpus and the Catala lineage, but the paper is heavier on PL/tooling and lighter on AI&Law-typical claims (legal reasoning evaluation, empirical user studies); reviewers there will press on the latter. **SLE** (Software Language Engineering) is a credible third option for the grammar-gap taxonomy. The current shape is **not** a strong fit for top-tier PL venues (PLDI, POPL, ICFP) — there is no formal soundness/completeness theorem, and the verification component is "engineering, not research" (the authors say so explicitly in `\sec:related`, `\subsec:rel_wider`).

- **Originality framing**: Originality is real but narrowly framed. The contribution is a *combination* (statute-shaped top-level + penalty algebra + burden qualifiers + causal/temporal edges + Catala-style priority + full-coverage corpus), not any individual primitive. The matrix in `\sec:related` (Table~\ref{tab:expressivity}) honestly admits each primitive exists somewhere; the Yuho row is the only one with all eight checks. This is a defensible "new point in the design space" framing, but the introduction occasionally drifts into stronger claims (e.g. `\sec:intro`: "the gap is mostly a grammar gap") that are not formally established.

- **Significance**: Moderate-to-high for the legal-informatics community; moderate for PL. The 524-section full-coverage encoding is the single most significant artefact — no peer system in the comparison matrix has demonstrated comparable scale on a production penal code. The grammar-gap classification ("genuine grammar gap" vs "diagnostic-layer issue masquerading as grammar gap") is a transferable methodological insight that could be cited by future legal-DSL builders.

- **Scope vs. depth**: Scope is too broad for the depth currently achieved. The paper covers grammar design, AST, six transpilers, Z3+Alloy verification, LSP, MCP server, VS Code extension, fidelity diagnostics, three-tier coverage harness, and throughput measurement in 8 sections. Each is sketched; few are interrogated. `\sec:implementation` reads as a tour rather than an analysis. Either drop the surface area (fold LSP/MCP/VSCode into a single "Editor surfaces" subsection and cite the repo for detail) or pick 2--3 components and go deep (recommended: grammar + fidelity diagnostics + the gap taxonomy).

- **Audience clarity (who is the reader)**: Mixed. The introduction and design sections speak to a PL/SE audience comfortable with grammars and ASTs; the background and related-work sections speak to an AI&Law audience familiar with Catala and Akoma Ntoso; the limitations section speaks to lawyers ("L3 stamp should be read as ..."). The paper does not commit to a primary reader. For Onward!, a stronger commitment to the PL/SE reader would help; for ICAIL/JURIX, the legal-content depth needs to come up.

## 3. Strengths

- **Honest scope and calibration.** `\sec:intro` ("What this paper is not"), `\subsec:threats`, and `\sec:limitations` are unusually candid for a preprint. The authors explicitly disclaim legal-advice status, flag that the L3 reviewer is the encoder, and admit diagnostic precision is unestablished. This is editorially valuable — it pre-empts the reviewer attacks that typically sink legal-DSL papers.

- **The grammar-gap taxonomy is a genuine contribution.** `\subsec:gaps` (Table~\ref{tab:gaps}) and `\subsec:gaps_eval` distinguish "genuine grammar limitation", "not-a-gap", "fidelity-linter issue", and "deferred semantic resolver". The observation that "a gap that looks like a grammar problem may, on inspection, be a linting problem in disguise --- and conflating the two leads to grammar bloat with no corresponding gain in fidelity" (`\sec:intro`, restated `\subsec:gaps`) is a quotable methodological point.

- **Full-corpus coverage on a production statute is rare.** The 524/524 encoding (`\subsec:coverage_eval`), with per-section directory layout and metadata.toml, is concrete and reproducible. The honest comparison in Table~\ref{tab:tooling} ("Catala: fragments of FR tax code; lam4: synthetic contracts; LexScript: various") is well-supported and gives Yuho a defensible territorial claim.

- **Comparison matrix is well-constructed.** Tables~\ref{tab:expressivity} and~\ref{tab:tooling} in `\sec:related` are the strongest editorial artefacts in the paper. The decision to mark "$\sim$" rather than "\checkmark" where documentation does not pin a feature down is the right scholarly posture.

- **Fail-fast engineering posture is visible in the artefact.** The paper backs its claims with concrete repository pointers (`library/penal_code/`, `scripts/paper_methodology.py`, `paper/methodology/fidelity_hits.json`), and the build pipeline injects coverage statistics into the manuscript at compile time (`\sec:conclusion`). Reproducibility is real.

## 4. Weaknesses

- **The L3 coverage figure is rhetorically inconsistent.** `\subsec:coverage_eval` reports "524/524 (100%) carry an L3 human-verified fidelity audit". `\sec:limitations` ("L3 reviewer is the encoder") concludes with "The 23\% L3 coverage figure is honest in this respect". These two numbers cannot both be in the paper without explanation. Either the limitations paragraph is a fossilised draft, or the 100% L3 figure has not been updated everywhere. **Action**: reconcile both passages and explain the unit (sections stamped vs sections externally reviewed). This is the single most important fix before submission to any peer-reviewed venue.

- **The contribution relative to LexScript is under-argued.** `\sec:related`, `\subsec:rel_lexscript` characterises LexScript as "thinner-grammar / broader-coverage" vs Yuho's "richer-grammar / single-family". This is a philosophical positioning, not an empirical comparison. The reader does not learn what LexScript can or cannot encode that Yuho can, with a worked example. Given LexScript shares the tree-sitter pipeline shape and is the closest tooling peer, the paper should include at least one side-by-side encoding fragment. Currently the LexScript citation has a placeholder `note` field in `references.bib` ("Project documentation; treat the public source tree as canonical") which compounds the under-substantiation.

- **Formal claims are hedged but not always labelled.** `\sec:intro` says "every section that the grammar could not natively express is documented as a numbered grammar gap"; `\sec:limitations` correctly admits "we report 524/524 as a coverage statistic rather than a soundness theorem". The intermediate sections occasionally slip into stronger phrasing — e.g. `\subsec:exceptions` "The compilation target is Z3 prioritised rewriting in the style of Catala" without showing the rewriting algorithm or asserting any property that is preserved. A reader expecting Catala-equivalent soundness will be disappointed; flag this earlier.

- **The "engineering exercise" framing risks undercutting the paper.** `\sec:intro` and `\sec:conclusion` both close with a variant of "closing the gap [...] is mostly an engineering exercise once the grammar is right". For Onward! this is fine (essay-style). For TPJ/SLE/ICAIL it reads as the authors arguing their own work is non-research. **Action**: reframe as "the empirical claim of this paper is that an appropriately-shaped grammar dissolves [a class of] previously-reported encoding obstacles" — keep the substance, lose the self-deprecation.

- **Evaluation conflates different units of merit.** `\sec:evaluation` reports coverage (524/524), gap-frequency (G6/G8/G12 dominate), fidelity-diagnostic hits (98/48/2/208), and throughput (1-day median). The gap-frequency analysis has no denominator stated for the "approximately one section in three" claim about G6 (`\subsec:gaps_eval`). The fidelity-diagnostic hit-rate is admitted to be unvalidated ("true-positive rates are not yet measured", `\subsec:threats`). The throughput numbers (median=1 day, p95=1 day) are obviously degenerate (`paper/methodology/methodology.tex` confirms `\statThroughputMedian{}` = `\statThroughputP95{}` = 1). **Action**: either drop throughput as a metric or replace with a real distribution; and run the 30-warning hand-rated spot-check before submission so `\subsec:fidelity_eval` has confirmed numbers.

## 5. Specific Comments

1. **`\sec:intro`, paragraph 4 "Contributions"**: Four contributions are listed but they are not parallel in shape. Contribution 1 is a language artefact, 2 is a corpus, 3 is tooling, 4 is empirical findings. Consider re-ordering as artefact (grammar+corpus together), tooling, methodology (gap taxonomy), in three contributions. The four-bullet enumeration weakens the claim that the gap taxonomy is the real intellectual contribution.

2. **`\sec:intro`, "the gap is mostly a grammar gap"**: This is the paper's headline thesis. The introduction asserts it; the rest of the paper does not directly test it. Suggest moving this from a declarative claim to a research question ("we investigate whether the persistent statute-encoding gap reduces, in practice, to a grammar gap") and let the gap catalogue answer it. The current framing reads as a conclusion before the data arrives.

3. **`\subsec:s415`, Figure~\ref{fig:s415}**: The worked example is illustrative but does not exercise exceptions, case law, or amendment lineage — three of the primitives the paper argues are distinctive. Add a second worked example (s302 is referenced repeatedly elsewhere; promote it to a figure) that uses nested penalty combinators, the death-penalty `when` conditional, and at least one Chapter IV exception cross-reference. This would make the contribution claims concrete.

4. **`\subsec:gaps`, Table~\ref{tab:gaps}**: The "Resolution" column has four values (fixed / not-a-gap / lint / deferred). Add a fifth column "Frequency" with the encoder-time-spent ranking that `\subsec:gaps_eval` describes only narratively. A grammar-gap taxonomy without per-gap frequency data is a list, not an empirical contribution.

5. **`\sec:implementation`, `\subsec:transpilers`**: "The Alloy transpiler is the largest of the six ($\sim$26k SLOC)" is striking and unexplained. Either justify why Alloy emission requires 26k SLOC against a $\sim$900-SLOC grammar (six lines of grammar drive 26k lines of emission seems disproportionate; is the figure correct?) or remove the number. Cross-check against Table~\ref{tab:sloc} which reports "Transpilers (six targets) 4,200" total — 26k for one transpiler contradicts a 4.2k aggregate.

6. **`\subsec:throughput`, "Phase~D agent regime processed all 524 sections in $\sim$3.5 hours [...] across four parallel codex-5 instances at high reasoning"**: The paper does not discuss what role the LLM played in *authoring* (vs *reviewing*) the encodings, and whether L3 stamps from the same agent population that did the encoding constitute an independent check. This is the same conflation the L3-reviewer-is-the-encoder limitation flags, but at the agent layer rather than the human layer. Add an explicit paragraph stating which agents/humans did encoding vs review and whether they were the same population.

7. **`\sec:related`, `\subsec:rel_catala`**: The disagreement-on-top-level-shape argument (scope vs statute) is the most substantive PL-side claim in the paper. It deserves a dedicated half-page (currently ~12 lines). Sketch a hypothetical Catala encoding of s415 alongside the Yuho encoding to make the structural mismatch concrete; this would also strengthen Specific Comment 3.

8. **`\sec:limitations`, "No formal grammar-completeness theorem"**: The paragraph correctly disclaims a formal theorem. But the paper would be stronger with a *claim* about what the gap catalogue rules out — e.g. "no Penal Code section currently in force triggers a grammar rejection at L1; a successor amendment introducing a novel drafting pattern would surface as a fifteenth gap". State the empirical falsifier explicitly.

## 6. Minor Comments

- **References.bib placeholders.** Four entries (`lam4`, `lexscript`, `ldoc`, `hammond1983rights`) carry placeholder `note` fields. `lexscript` lacks an author and a stable URL; `ldoc` lacks any locator. For arXiv this is tolerable; for any peer-reviewed venue these must resolve to citable artefacts (preprint, repo permalink, or technical report). The `lessig1999code` entry is in the bibliography but I did not see it cited in any section file — drop or cite.
- **`\sec:abstract`**: "six transpilers (JSON, controlled English, \LaTeX{}, Mermaid, Alloy, DOCX)" — `main.tex` and `\sec:implementation` agree on six, but the project memory and CLAUDE.md context refer to 13 targets. If the additional transpilers (jsonld, prolog, akomantoso, etc.) exist, decide whether the paper's "six" is the curated subset and say so explicitly; otherwise the artefact-paper mismatch will be flagged.
- **Terminology nit**: the paper uses "L3" for both the coverage tier and the human-stamp procedure. `\subsec:coverage` defines both senses but they drift. Pick one and footnote the other.
- **`\subsec:gaps_eval`**: "approximately one section in three" needs a number. From `library/penal_code/` this should be a count, not an estimate.
- **`\subsec:throughput`**: "four parallel \texttt{gpt-5.4} instances at \texttt{high} reasoning" — model names in the artefact register matter for reproducibility; if `gpt-5.4` is the agent model, cite the vendor's release announcement.
- **Figure rendering**: `\sec:design` references three figures (`s415`, `penalty-tree`, `exception-priority`); `\sec:implementation` references `architecture`. Confirm all four are present in `paper/figures/` before submission.
- **Title vs subtitle tension**: The subtitle "Grammar, Tooling, and a 524-Section Proof of Concept" promises three contributions; the body delivers four. Align.
- **`\sec:conclusion`**: The Catala-style scope-composition strand (final paragraph) is hedged as "more speculative", but the design section already commits to scope composition as an additional layer in `\subsec:statute`. Make the future-work paragraph match.

## 7. Recommendation

- **Decision**: **Major Revision** (for any peer-reviewed venue). For arXiv preprint as-is, the paper is already postable, but I would strongly recommend the L3 reconciliation (Weakness 1) and the SLOC reconciliation (Specific Comment 5) before posting, because both are objective inconsistencies that any careful reader will notice and that damage reviewer trust.

- **Confidence**: 4/5. I have read the paper top-to-bottom and the supporting `methodology.tex`, `references.bib`, and `main.tex`. I have not run the build pipeline or inspected the encoded library directly; my reading of the engineering numbers is paper-internal.

- **Per-dimension scores (0--100)**:
  - **Originality**: 70 — the combination is genuinely new; individual primitives are not.
  - **Significance**: 65 — moderate-to-high for legal-informatics, moderate for PL.
  - **Clarity**: 70 — well-written, but the four-contribution framing and the L3 inconsistency hurt.
  - **Soundness**: 55 — empirical claims are honestly hedged but the gap-frequency and fidelity-precision numbers need backing; the L3 figure has internal contradictions.
  - **Reproducibility**: 80 — repository, methodology scripts, and statistics-injection pipeline are exemplary.

- **Rationale**: This is a thoughtful, well-scoped engineering paper with one genuinely transferable methodological insight (the gap-vs-linter taxonomy) and a substantive empirical artefact (full-coverage Penal Code encoding). The paper's instinct to be honest about limitations is a strength, but it is undermined by two internal inconsistencies (the 100%-vs-23% L3 figure and the 26k-vs-4.2k transpiler SLOC figure) that would be flagged by any careful peer reviewer and that erode the paper's credibility on the rest of its quantitative claims. With a focused major revision that (a) reconciles the L3 numbers, (b) puts a frequency column on the gap table, (c) runs the spot-check on fidelity diagnostics, and (d) adds one comparative encoding fragment against Catala or LexScript, this paper is a credible Onward! Essays / TPJ / SLE submission. As an arXiv preprint, it is publishable now after the two consistency fixes.
