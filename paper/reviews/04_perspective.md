# Reviewer 3 Report — Cross-Disciplinary Perspective

**Reviewer**: PL/DSL designer (Onward!/SLE/GPCE perspective)
**Manuscript**: Yuho: A DSL for the Singapore Penal Code

## 1. Summary

The paper presents Yuho, an external DSL for encoding Singapore's Penal Code 1871 as machine-checkable artefacts. The contribution is presented as four-fold: (i) the grammar (statute-shaped top-level with named fields for definitions, elements, penalty, illustrations, exceptions, case law, parties, amendment lineage), (ii) a complete encoding of all 524 sections with three-tier coverage (L1 parse / L2 build+lint / L3 human-stamped), (iii) a tooling stack (tree-sitter parser, Python AST, six transpilers, Z3+Alloy verification hookup, LSP, MCP server, VS Code extension), and (iv) an empirical catalogue of 14 grammar gaps surfaced during mass-encoding, classified into genuine grammar limitations (10) and diagnostic-layer issues (4). Total tooling: 38.7k SLOC; corpus: 16.4k SLOC.

The paper reads as a careful, honest, well-written engineering report. The authorial voice is unusually self-aware about its own genre: the limitations and threats-to-validity sections concede single-jurisdiction scope, encoder=L3-reviewer entanglement, and unestablished diagnostic precision before a reviewer raises them. From a PL/DSL research perspective, however, the question this report does not adequately answer is also the headline one: what is the *language-design* contribution that an SLE/Onward!/GPCE audience could not have inferred from the existence of Catala plus tree-sitter plus a tax-code-shaped re-skinning?

## 2. The "So What?" Test

The four contributions in §sec:intro deserve to be challenged individually.

**Contribution 1 — the grammar.** The claimed novelty is a statute-shaped top-level with deontic element types, burden qualifiers, penalty algebra, Catala-style exceptions, causal/temporal element edges, and amendment lineage. From a PL standpoint, every primitive on this list is borrowed: deontic operators from LegalRuleML; priority-default exceptions from Catala (the paper explicitly says so); causal/temporal annotations are common in process-modelling DSLs; statute-shaped struct-of-named-fields is the design pattern Akoma Ntoso has been shipping for over a decade. The novelty claim therefore reduces to *the specific combination plus the named-field shape plus the doc-comment lifting*. That is engineering execution, not language-design research. The paper would benefit from being explicit about this — currently it leans on novelty rhetoric ("none of these target a common-law penal code at full coverage") that conflates *target-domain* novelty with *language-design* novelty.

**Contribution 2 — the complete encoding.** This is the real artefact contribution and the strongest leg of the paper. 524/524 at L1+L2 with all sections L3-stamped is a non-trivial engineering result and a useful corpus for the field. But again, from a PL-research perspective, *encoding a corpus is not a language-design finding*. It is the empirical input from which language-design findings would be extracted.

**Contribution 3 — the tooling.** LSP and MCP and six transpilers are valuable engineering. The paper's own §subsec:rel_wider concedes the verification stack borrows from formal methods literature and "our use of them is engineering rather than research". By the paper's own framing, contribution 3 is engineering, not research.

**Contribution 4 — the gap catalogue and the genuine-vs-diagnostic distinction.** This is the most defensible *PL-research* contribution in the paper, and §subsec:gaps does articulate it well: the claim is that mass-encoding surfaces a catalogue of grammar shortcomings, and that distinguishing "genuine grammar gap requiring a parser change" from "fidelity issue masquerading as a grammar gap" is methodologically important. This is genuinely interesting and could be the basis of a stronger paper — but the paper does not promote it to that level. It is buried as one of four contributions and presented in tabular form (Table 2) that reads as a development log.

**Net "so what?":** what does the field gain from Yuho that it didn't have? The defensible answer is: (a) a public 524-section corpus for benchmarking other legal DSLs, (b) a methodological observation about the gap-vs-lint distinction, and (c) a worked existence proof that one can mass-encode a common-law penal code. (a) and (c) are artefact contributions; (b) is the language-design contribution and deserves more weight than the paper currently gives it.

## 3. Strengths

1. **Honesty.** The L3-reviewer-is-the-encoder caveat, the diagnostic-precision-unestablished caveat, and the "no formal grammar-completeness theorem" caveat are all surfaced in §sec:limitations and §subsec:threats. A surprising number of legal-DSL papers in adjacent venues do not do this.
2. **Engineering completeness.** Six transpile targets, LSP, MCP, Z3, Alloy, fidelity diagnostics, three-tier coverage, agent-driven re-encoding pipeline. The artefact is real and the SLOC numbers (38.7k impl) are credible for the claimed surface area.
3. **The corpus.** 524/524 is the headline. As benchmark infrastructure for *other* legal-DSL researchers this is more valuable than the paper itself probably realises — it is what subsequent papers will cite.
4. **The gap-vs-lint methodological observation.** Genuinely interesting, under-promoted; see §6 below.
5. **MCP integration.** Possibly the first legal DSL to ship MCP, and the dual LSP+MCP framing of "one analyser stack, two consumer surfaces" is the kind of design observation Onward! likes.

## 4. Weaknesses

1. **The novelty/engineering line is blurred.** The paper claims four contributions of which two (grammar, tooling) are largely engineering execution of borrowed primitives; the borrowing is acknowledged in §subsec:rel_catala etc., but the introduction's contribution list does not flag which contributions are which kind.
2. **Fidelity diagnostic precision/recall are unestablished.** §subsec:fidelity_eval reports raw warning counts (98 / 48 / 2 / 208) and explicitly notes "true-positive rates are not yet measured". This is a serious gap for an evaluation section. The paper's own footnote that the disjunctive-connective check (208 hits) probably has poor precision is unusually candid but still unanswered.
3. **Single-jurisdiction.** Deferred to §sec:limitations and §sec:conclusion. Without it, contribution (1) is "a grammar that fits *one* statute"; the paper's portability claim ("plausibly portable across the wider Penal Code family") is explicitly empirically unverified.
4. **The grammar-design choices are under-justified.** Why these named fields and not user-defined sections? Why a statute-shaped top-level instead of a Catala scope with metadata fields? The §subsec:rel_catala disagreement is asserted, not argued (see §6 below).
5. **The verification stack does relatively little for the user.** 3.3k SLOC of Z3+Alloy hookup buys exception-priority well-formedness checking, bounded element-combination enumeration, and pairwise inter-section conflict detection. A skeptic would ask: do users actually run these? What questions do they answer that a structural lint pass would not? See §6 thought experiment.

## 5. Cross-Disciplinary Connections

The paper engages with the legal-DSL literature (Catala, lam4, LexScript, LDOC, Akoma Ntoso, LegalRuleML) and with formal methods (Z3, Alloy). It does not engage with several PL-research literatures that would strengthen its conceptual framing:

- **Language workbenches and projectional editing (MPS, Spoofax, Rascal).** The named-field grammar shape, the doc-comment-as-AST-attribute lift, the multi-target transpilation pattern, and the LSP+MCP dual-surface architecture are all classic language-workbench moves. The paper should at minimum cite Voelter's work on projectional editing and the Spoofax body of work (Visser et al.) on declarative language definition. For a domain where the *user* is a lawyer, projectional editing is arguably a better fit than text-based external DSLs, and the paper's silence on this design alternative is striking.
- **Bidirectional transformations / lenses.** The "encoded artefact is canonical, downstream renderings are generated" posture in §sec:conclusion is a one-way transformation. Foster et al.'s lens framework would let the author argue rigorously for the asymmetry — *why* must the .yh file be the source of truth? Could a bidirectional Word↔Yuho lens give LDOC's authoring posture and Yuho's verification posture simultaneously?
- **Model-driven engineering / Ecore / EMF.** The "AST is a struct of named fields, every visitor consumes the same AST, transpilers and verifiers are visitors" pattern *is* MDE. The paper would benefit from at least noting that the architectural shape is not novel.
- **IDE-as-IO and "Languages as a Service".** Erdweg et al.'s state-of-the-art-of-language-workbenches survey (2013, 2015) is the literature this paper most needs to engage. The 6-transpiler / LSP / MCP / verification combination is a language-workbench feature matrix in all but name.
- **Internal vs. external DSL tradeoffs.** Why an external DSL with a tree-sitter grammar, instead of an embedded DSL in Python/Racket/Scala? The cost of external DSLs is well-documented (you give up a host language's ecosystem); the benefit (a syntax that lawyers can read) is asserted but not measured. Has any lawyer read a .yh file?
- **The DSL-economy literature.** Mernik/Heering/Sloane (2005, ACM CSUR) and the SLE literature on when-to-build-a-DSL would let the paper engage Reviewer 3's contribution-level "so what?" rigorously rather than rhetorically.
- **Akoma Ntoso processing literature.** There is a body of work on Akoma-Ntoso-derived semantics (Palmirani et al.) that the paper cites Akoma Ntoso for the standard but not for the processing scholarship.

## 6. Specific Perspective Comments

1. **(§sec:design top-level shape) The named-field positions are over-specified.** Definitions, elements, penalty, illustration, exception, caselaw, parties, subsection — eight positions hard-wired into the grammar. What does the user pay if a future amendment introduces a ninth structural field type ("commencement notes", "transitional provisions", "pictorial illustrations")? In a Catala-with-metadata or an Akoma-Ntoso-like extensible-element design, a new field type is a schema extension. In Yuho, it is a grammar.js change. The paper should be explicit about this tradeoff and quantify how often it has bitten the project — gaps G1, G3, G5, G6, G8, G9, G12, G14 are arguably all instances of this cost being paid.

2. **(§subsec:rel_catala) The scope-vs-statute disagreement is asserted, not argued.** Catala scopes can carry metadata and structured fields; nothing in Catala's design *forbids* attaching illustrations, definitions, or caselaw to a scope. The paper's claim is that a function-call shape "does not naturally accommodate" these fields. Run the constructive thought experiment: a Catala scope `s415_cheating` with input parameters bound to elements, output bound to penalty, and metadata fields for illustration / definition / caselaw — would this not simulate a Yuho statute block exactly? If yes, the disagreement is presentation, not semantics. If no, the paper should show why.

3. **(§subsec:parser) Tree-sitter context-sensitivity.** Tree-sitter is intentionally weak on context-sensitivity (it does not enforce, for example, that an exception's `defeats <element_name>` references an element that exists in the same statute). The paper does not surface which constraints are pushed to the AST/lint layer. A reader interested in the design tradeoff would want a list: "the following constraints are enforced by the grammar: ...; the following are deferred to the AST validator: ...; the following to the lint layer: ...". G4 (illustration count) and G11 (or-vs-all_of) are explicitly lint-layer; G10 (cross-section resolution) is explicitly future. What about deontic-type-vs-element-kind compatibility? Causal-edge target validity? Burden-qualifier-vs-proof-standard pairing?

4. **(§subsec:gaps) The 14-gap catalogue is presented as an empirical finding.** It reads more naturally as a development log. If it is the methodological contribution (and §subsec:gaps does articulate the genuine-vs-diagnostic distinction usefully), promote it: the paper currently treats the gap classification as a side-observation in Table 2 rather than as the spine of the methodological argument. The gap catalogue would carry more PL-research weight if framed as "we propose a methodology — *gap classification by resolution mechanism* — that distinguishes grammar bloat from diagnostic enrichment, and apply it to a 524-section corpus".

5. **(§subsec:rel_wider, MCP) The first-legal-DSL-to-ship-MCP claim is engineering.** What would make it a *research* claim is articulating what specifically about a legal DSL benefits from an MCP surface that does not benefit from an LSP surface. The §subsec:editors text says "tools the model can call, resources the model can read, and prompts the host can pre-load" — this is the MCP spec. What is *Yuho-specific* about the MCP design? `yuho_section_pair` returning canonical-text-alongside-encoded-AST is interesting; `yuho_run_l3_review` invoking the L3 checklist is interesting. Promote these to design observations rather than tool-list bullets.

6. **(§sec:evaluation Table 2, SLOC ratios) The 38.7k:16.4k ratio is presented as a "fair quick check on DSL density".** As a PL-research measure, raw SLOC ratio is a poor proxy. Better measures: (a) tokens-per-statute-section in .yh vs. tokens-per-section in canonical text (compression vs. expansion), (b) cyclomatic complexity of typical .yh files, (c) reuse rate of tooling components when a new statute family is added (the §sec:limitations Indian Penal Code exercise would let the author measure this), (d) author throughput before/after each grammar gap fix. The paper's "between 1:1 and 2:1 is the productive range" heuristic is back-of-envelope; the SLE literature has more rigorous measures.

7. **(§subsec:verify thought experiment) Walking through the s299/s300 mutual-exclusion query.** A user asks: "are s299 (culpable homicide) and s300 (murder) mutually exclusive under my element bindings?" What does the verification stack actually do? From §subsec:verify: "given two sections that plausibly overlap (e.g.\ s299 and s300), the solver searches for an element-binding that satisfies both, and reports the configuration if one exists." Walk it through: (a) does the user provide the element bindings, or are they enumerated? (b) does the encoded mens-rea-degree distinction (intent to kill vs. knowledge of likelihood) survive into the Z3 model, or is it a free string? (c) what do the Chapter IV general exceptions do — are they composed automatically into both sides? (d) what is the witness format the solver returns — a binding to element variables, or natural-language? The paper does not show this query end-to-end. For a PL-research claim about verification utility, a worked end-to-end example is the minimum bar.

8. **(§subsec:editors LSP adoption) Has the LSP been used by anyone other than the author?** §subsec:tooling_eval says "the Language Server, the inlay hints, and context-aware completion were used during the entire Phase~D mass-encoding effort." This appears to mean the author used it during their own encoding work. External adoption — by lawyers, by legal-tech engineers, by anyone outside the project — is not reported. For an editor-surface contribution claim, single-user data is a thin basis.

9. **(§subsec:fidelity_eval, precision/recall) The diagnostic numbers are *raw warnings, not validated true positives*.** The paper concedes this. As a PL/SE engineering paper this is mid-grade (a rough indicator with hand-rating planned); as a research paper it is suspect. The hand-rated spot-check (30 warnings per check) listed on the punch-list is the work that would convert the section from "we built this and counted hits" to "we built this and measured precision". Without it, the §subsec:fidelity_eval claims "the fabricated-fine sentinel and corresponding diagnostic eliminated the class" use [Inference]-level "eliminated" without measurement.

10. **(§sec:design grammar choice) Why named-field positions and not a sections-record + extensible-tags model?** The grammar fixes eight named field types as first-class. An extensible design (an `attribute name=value` pattern with a small registry of well-known attribute names) would be more accommodating to drafting variation across jurisdictions. The fixed-shape choice is a conscious tradeoff but the paper does not justify it against the extensible alternative.

11. **(§subsec:rel_catala soundness theorem) "Catala formalises a soundness theorem ... we do not."** This is honest but it is also the difference between a PL-research paper and an engineering report. Catala's theorem is what makes Catala a PL-research artefact. Without an analogous formal claim, Yuho's contribution sits below the SLE/Onward! research bar regardless of corpus size. The paper should be candid about the difference.

12. **(§sec:conclusion future work, scope composition) The "scope composition layered on top of statute blocks" speculative strand is the most PL-research-shaped item in the paper.** A layered architecture where statute blocks are the structural primitive and scopes compose them as functions would let Yuho subsume Catala-shaped reasoning rather than diverge from it. This is the design observation Onward! would want as the paper's headline; it is a one-paragraph aside in the conclusion.

13. **(§subsec:transpilers Alloy 26k SLOC) The Alloy transpiler is 26k SLOC?** Table 2 says "Transpilers (six targets) 4,200" total. The text in §subsec:transpilers says "The Alloy transpiler is the largest of the six (~26k SLOC)". These numbers contradict — 26k > 4.2k. Either the text or the table is wrong. (Probably the text means 2.6k.)

14. **(§subsec:fidelity G11 hits 208 / 524 sections = 40%) A 40% flag rate on a heuristic the paper itself says will have low precision is operationally close to "warn on every section".** The paper acknowledges this. But it should also reflect on what the grammar-design implication is: if the structural ambiguity between "all_of with comma-separated elements" and "any_of with or-connected elements" cannot be resolved by a heuristic, perhaps the grammar should make encoders write the connective explicitly per element pair, eliminating the lint at the source. This would be a grammar-design response to a lint-layer signal — exactly the kind of feedback loop §subsec:gaps says distinguishes grammar gaps from diagnostic issues.

## 7. Practical Impact Assessment

**Reader profile.** The paper sits awkwardly between three communities: legal-DSL researchers (will value the Catala-comparison and the gap catalogue), PL/SLE researchers (will want a sharper language-design contribution and engagement with language-workbench literature), and legal-tech practitioners (will want the corpus and the tooling, may not care about the research framing). The paper currently writes for the third audience while claiming the first audience's contribution shape.

**Adoption picture.** The corpus will be cited by the legal-DSL community as benchmark infrastructure. The grammar will be adopted by zero one-shot users (external DSLs are sticky; lawyers will not learn yh syntax for a single statute). The MCP server is the most likely adoption surface — agent-based legal workflows are a current research wave, and a turnkey MCP server for a real penal code is useful infrastructure. The LSP will see negligible external use until at least one independent user reports success.

**What would convince a skeptic.** (a) An independent lawyer or legal-tech engineer reading and modifying a .yh file with positive ergonomic feedback. (b) A 50-section Indian Penal Code encoding showing tooling reuse and quantified grammar-extension cost. (c) Hand-rated precision figures for the four fidelity diagnostics (the §subsec:threats punch-list item). (d) A worked end-to-end verification query that a Z3-naive lawyer can read.

## 8. Fundamental Assumption Challenges

1. **Assumption: a textual external DSL is the right surface for legal encoding.** The paper does not consider projectional editing (MPS-style) or structured-form authoring. If the audience is lawyers, a syntax-free projectional editor on top of the same AST might give the verification benefits without the syntax-learning cost. *If questioned:* the entire grammar-design contribution would be reframed as schema design rather than language design, and tree-sitter would become an implementation detail rather than a feature.

2. **Assumption: statute-shape is a fundamental structural primitive that justifies a top-level construct.** Catala disagrees (scope is the primitive). Akoma Ntoso disagrees (a `<section>` is just one element among many). The paper's argument for statute-as-top-level is "it matches how lawyers read the document". But penal-code drafting is one specific drafting tradition; tax code, contract law, regulatory codes have different drafting shapes. *If questioned:* Yuho is not "a DSL for legal statute" but "a DSL for one drafting tradition's statutes", and the §sec:limitations cross-jurisdiction caveat applies more sharply than the paper currently concedes.

3. **Assumption: encoding-as-source-of-truth is the right posture.** §sec:conclusion stakes this as a longer-term thesis. But the canonical statute on Singapore Statutes Online is the legal source of truth — by Singapore law, by parliamentary process, by gazette. The .yh file is *derived* from the canonical text and re-rendered to it. Treating the .yh as canonical and the gazette as derived inverts the legal status of the artefacts. *If questioned:* Yuho is a *parallel encoding* with verification properties, not a source-of-truth replacement, and the paper's framing in §sec:conclusion overclaims.

4. **(Bonus) Assumption: SLOC is a meaningful measure of DSL economy.** §subsec: implementation 38.7k:16.4k ratio argument. Alternative measures (compression ratio, author throughput, tooling reuse rate) are not reported. *If questioned:* the "1:1-to-2:1 productive range" heuristic dissolves and the paper would need a different DSL-economy argument.

5. **(Bonus) Assumption: the gap catalogue generalises beyond Singapore.** G1–G14 are the gaps that surfaced for *one* Penal Code. A different statute family would surface a different gap set. The paper's implicit claim that the methodology (gap classification by resolution mechanism) is generalisable is plausible but not demonstrated. *If questioned:* the catalogue becomes a Singapore-specific development log rather than a generalisable PL-research finding.

## 9. Recommendation

- **Decision:** Major revision.
- **Confidence:** 4/5.
- **Per-dimension scores (0–100):**
  - Originality (PL framing): 45 (primitives are borrowed; novel contribution is the gap-classification methodology, currently under-promoted)
  - Practical Impact: 65 (corpus is durable infrastructure; tooling is real but adoption-untested)
  - Conceptual Depth: 50 (engineering-thick, design-argument-thin; design choices asserted not derived)
  - Engagement with PL Literature: 35 (legal-DSL literature engaged competently; language-workbench, MDE, BX, projectional-editing literatures absent)
  - Cross-Disciplinary Reach: 60 (good legal-CS bridge; weaker PL-CS bridge; MCP integration is the cross-disciplinary bright spot)

**Rationale.** The artefact is real, the engineering is honest, and the corpus is a durable contribution to the legal-DSL community. As a PL/DSL research paper for an SLE/Onward!/GPCE audience the contribution shape is mismatched: three of four claimed contributions are engineering execution of borrowed primitives, the one genuinely PL-research-shaped contribution (the gap-vs-lint methodological distinction) is under-promoted, and the design rationale for the grammar's specific shape is asserted rather than argued against constructively-considered alternatives (Catala-with-metadata, language-workbench / projectional editing, extensible-attribute models). The fidelity-diagnostic precision is unestablished, the LSP adoption is single-user, and the cross-jurisdictional generalisation is deferred. A major revision that (a) reframes the gap-classification methodology as the headline PL contribution, (b) hand-rates a precision sample of the fidelity diagnostics, (c) engages the language-workbench / MDE / BX literatures, and (d) either ships a 50-section Indian Penal Code pilot or downgrades the portability claim would substantially strengthen the paper's standing in a PL-research venue. As-is it reads as a strong engineering report whose research framing is one revision short of where it needs to be.
